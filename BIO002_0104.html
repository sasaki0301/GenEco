Irritability and Adaptability in Organisms

Living things can detect and respond to changes in their surroundings — this is called irritability. Over time, organisms also develop adaptations that help them survive and thrive in different environments.

1. Irritability — The Power to Respond

Irritability is the ability of living organisms to detect changes (stimuli) and respond appropriately. These responses help organisms survive, avoid harm, and maintain stability.

Example: a cat reacts to sound, or a plant bends toward light.

2. Irritability in Plants — Tropisms

Plants respond slowly to environmental stimuli by changing their direction of growth. These movements are known as tropisms and are usually controlled by plant hormones such as auxins.

  • Phototropism: Growth toward light (e.g., stems bending toward sunlight).
  • Geotropism (Gravitropism): Growth in response to gravity (e.g., roots growing downward).
  • Hydrotropism: Growth toward moisture (e.g., roots seeking water).
  • Thigmotropism: Response to touch (e.g., vines wrapping around supports).

Plant growth responses toward or away from stimuli are called tropisms.

Activity 1: Identify the Type of Tropism

A bean plant bends toward sunlight through the window. What type of tropism does this show?

Answer: Phototropism — the plant grows toward the light source.

3. Irritability in Animals — Taxis and Reflex Actions

Animals can move quickly in response to stimuli. This movement toward or away from a stimulus is called taxis. Reflexes are automatic and immediate responses to protect the organism from harm.

  • Phototaxis: Movement in response to light (e.g., moths flying toward light).
  • Geotaxis: Movement in response to gravity (e.g., insects avoiding downward surfaces).
  • Chemotaxis: Movement toward or away from chemicals (e.g., sperm moving toward egg).
  • Thigmotaxis: Response to touch or surface contact (e.g., cockroaches hiding in crevices).

Animals move toward or away from stimuli — a key survival behavior.

Activity 2: Compare Responses

A sunflower turns toward the sun, while a moth flies toward a lamp. Which one shows tropism and which shows taxis?

The sunflower shows tropism (growth movement), while the moth shows taxis (active movement).

4. Adaptability — Surviving in a Changing World

Adaptation is the ability of organisms to adjust or develop traits that help them survive in specific environments. These can be structural, behavioral, or physiological.

  • Structural Adaptation: Physical traits that aid survival. Examples: Thick fur in polar bears, spines in cacti, webbed feet in ducks.
  • Behavioral Adaptation: Actions that improve survival. Examples: Bird migration, nocturnal hunting, hibernation.
  • Physiological Adaptation: Internal body processes that aid survival. Examples: Camels producing concentrated urine, snakes producing venom.

Adaptations may be physical, behavioral, or internal — each ensures survival and reproduction.

Activity 3: Identify the Adaptation Type

Penguins huddle together during winter to keep warm. What type of adaptation is this?

Answer: Behavioral adaptation — a cooperative action to reduce heat loss.

7. Lesson Summary

  • Irritability allows organisms to detect and respond to stimuli for survival.
  • Plants show tropisms (growth responses), while animals show taxis or reflexes (movement responses).
  • Adaptations can be structural, behavioral, or physiological — all improve survival chances.
  • Responses are short-term adjustments; adaptations are long-term evolutionary changes.
