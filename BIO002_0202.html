Lesson 2: Mendel’s First Law — Law of Dominance

Gregor Mendel’s experiments revealed that traits are not blended together but are controlled by discrete units — what we now call genes. His Law of Dominance explains why one trait can mask another when both are inherited.

1. Mendel’s Observations on Pea Plants

In his experiments, Mendel crossed pea plants with different traits — for example, tall (TT) and short (tt). He observed that all the first-generation (F₁) offspring were tall.

This result showed that one trait (tallness) dominated the other (shortness). Mendel called the stronger trait dominant and the hidden one recessive.

When tall (TT) plants were crossed with short (tt), all offspring were tall (Tt).

Activity 1: Observe Mendel’s Cross

What trait was dominant in Mendel’s tall × short pea plant experiment, and what happened to the short trait?

The tall trait was dominant, and the short trait was recessive (hidden in the F₁ generation).

2. Law of Dominance Explained

Mendel’s Law of Dominance states that when two organisms with contrasting traits are crossed, the trait that appears in the first generation (F₁) is the dominant trait, while the one that is hidden is the recessive trait.

Dominant alleles mask the expression of recessive alleles. Only when two recessive alleles pair (e.g., tt) does the recessive trait appear.

GenotypePhenotypeExplanation
TTTallBoth alleles are dominant.
TtTallDominant T masks the recessive t.
ttShortNo dominant allele present, recessive trait expressed.

Activity 2: Think and Explain

If a brown-eyed (BB) parent and a blue-eyed (bb) parent have children, what eye color will the offspring have?

All offspring will have brown eyes (Bb) because brown is dominant over blue.

3. Punnett Square Demonstration

A Punnett Square is a simple diagram used to predict the possible genetic outcomes of a cross.

Let’s explore three examples below:

Example 1: Tall (TT) × Short (tt)

All offspring are Tt → 100% Tall (dominant trait).

Example 2: Heterozygous Tall (Tt) × Heterozygous Tall (Tt)

Genotypes: TT (25%), Tt (50%), tt (25%) → Phenotypes: 75% Tall, 25% Short.

Example 3: Brown (Bb) × Blue (bb) Eyes

Genotypes: Bb (50%), bb (50%) → Phenotypes: 50% Brown, 50% Blue.

5. Summary Table — Dominant vs. Recessive Traits

  • Dominant traits appear in offspring even if only one dominant allele is present.
  • Recessive traits appear only when both alleles are recessive.
  • Punnett squares help predict genotype and phenotype ratios.
