Nutrition and Gas Exchange

All living organisms need energy to survive. This energy is obtained from nutrients and oxygen — both essential for growth, repair, and maintaining life processes.

1. The Importance of Nutrition and Gas Exchange

Nutrition provides materials for building body structures and generating energy. Gas exchange supplies oxygen for cellular respiration and removes carbon dioxide, a waste product. These processes work together to sustain life.

Oxygen enters organisms to release energy from food molecules.

2. Types of Nutrition

Organisms differ in how they obtain food and nutrients:

  • Autotrophic nutrition: Organisms like plants make their own food through photosynthesis using sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water.
  • Heterotrophic nutrition: Organisms like animals and fungi rely on other organisms for food.

3. Types of Heterotrophic Nutrition

Heterotrophs use different methods to obtain nutrients from other sources:

  • Holozoic nutrition: Organisms ingest solid food, digest it internally, and absorb nutrients. Example: Humans and most animals.
  • Saprophytic nutrition: Organisms feed on dead or decaying matter by secreting digestive enzymes and absorbing the nutrients. Example: Fungi and some bacteria.
  • Parasitic nutrition: Organisms live on or inside a host and obtain nutrients at the host’s expense. Example: Tapeworms, lice, mistletoe plants.

Activity 1: Identify the Nutrition Type

Which type of heterotrophic nutrition does a mushroom exhibit?

Answer: Saprophytic — it feeds on decaying matter by absorbing dissolved nutrients.

4. Gas Exchange in Plants and Animals

Gas exchange enables organisms to take in oxygen and release carbon dioxide for cellular respiration. In plants, gases enter and exit through tiny pores called stomata. In animals, exchange occurs in specialized structures like lungs, gills, or skin.

Photosynthesis and respiration complement each other — maintaining atmospheric balance.

Activity 2: Analyze the Balance

Explain how the gas exchange in plants and animals are opposite yet interdependent.

Plants use carbon dioxide and release oxygen during photosynthesis, while animals use oxygen and release carbon dioxide during respiration. Each provides the gases the other needs — maintaining a balanced ecosystem.

5. Gas Exchange in Humans

Humans exchange gases in the lungs, specifically in tiny air sacs called alveoli. Oxygen diffuses into the bloodstream, while carbon dioxide diffuses out to be exhaled.

Alveoli have thin walls and large surface areas, allowing efficient diffusion of gases.

7. Lesson Summary

  • Nutrition and gas exchange are essential for supplying energy and removing waste gases.
  • Autotrophs produce their own food, while heterotrophs depend on others.
  • Heterotrophic nutrition includes holozoic (ingestion), saprophytic (decomposition), and parasitic (host-dependent) modes.
  • Plants and animals exchange gases differently, but their processes are interlinked through photosynthesis and respiration.
