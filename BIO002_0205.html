Chapter 2 Summary Mendelian Genetics

Let’s review what you’ve learned about Genetics and Mendel’s Laws — from the basic building blocks of heredity to the patterns of inheritance discovered by Gregor Mendel.

1. Key Concepts Recap

  • Genetics – The study of heredity and variation.
  • Heredity – Transmission of traits from parents to offspring.
  • Gene – A segment of DNA that codes for a trait.
  • Alleles – Different forms of a gene (e.g., R and r).
  • Genotype – The genetic makeup (e.g., TT, Tt, or tt).
  • Phenotype – The physical expression (e.g., tall or short).

Genes on chromosomes control traits passed from parent to offspring.

2. Mendel’s Three Laws of Inheritance

LawDescriptionExample
Law of Dominance One allele can mask another; the dominant trait appears. TT or Tt = Tall, tt = Short
Law of Segregation Each trait is controlled by two alleles that separate during gamete formation. Tt → produces gametes T and t
Law of Independent Assortment Alleles for different traits assort independently, leading to genetic variation. Seed color and seed shape inherited separately

3. Practice Activities

Activity 1: Identify the Law

If a plant that is Tt produces gametes T and t, which Mendelian law is being demonstrated?

Law of Segregation – Alleles separate during gamete formation so each gamete carries only one.

Activity 2: Predict the Phenotype

In a cross between RrYy × RrYy, what is the expected ratio of phenotypes?

9:3:3:1 — 9 Round Yellow, 3 Round Green, 3 Wrinkled Yellow, 1 Wrinkled Green.

Activity 3: Human Example

Two heterozygous parents (Ff × Ff) for free earlobes have a child. What is the probability that the child has attached earlobes?

25% or 1/4 chance — only ff results in attached earlobes.

5. Key Terms Summary

  • Homozygous – Two identical alleles (TT or tt)
  • Heterozygous – Two different alleles (Tt)
  • Dihybrid Cross – A cross involving two traits
  • Gamete – Sex cell (sperm or egg) carrying one allele per gene
  • Phenotypic Ratio – Observable trait ratio (e.g., 3:1)
