Characteristics of Living Things

Living things share a set of characteristics that distinguish them from nonliving matter. These traits define life and help scientists understand how organisms function and interact.

1. What Makes Something Alive?

Biologists use specific criteria to identify whether something is living. A single-celled bacterium and a massive tree may look different, but both display the same essential characteristics of life.

2. Seven Major Characteristics of Life

CharacteristicDescription
1. OrganizationLiving things have a complex but ordered structure.
2. MetabolismThey obtain and use energy to carry out life processes.
3. HomeostasisThey maintain stable internal conditions despite external changes.
4. Growth and DevelopmentThey grow by increasing cell size or number and follow patterns coded in DNA.
5. ReproductionThey produce offspring to continue their species, either sexually or asexually.
6. Response to StimuliThey react to environmental changes (light, temperature, sound, etc.).
7. Adaptation through EvolutionOver generations, species change to better survive in their environment.

3. Interactive Activities

Activity 1: Identify the Characteristic

A sunflower turns toward sunlight every morning. Which characteristic of life does this show?

via GIPHY

Answer: Response to stimuli — the sunflower reacts to light by turning toward it.

Activity 2: Critical Thinking

A car can move and use energy (fuel), but it is not alive. Which characteristics of life does it lack?

It lacks growth, reproduction, and adaptation. Cars do not have cells or DNA — they cannot reproduce or evolve.

Activity 3: Compare and Contrast

Both bacteria and humans reproduce and respond to stimuli. How do they differ in their level of organization?

Bacteria are unicellular (one cell), while humans are multicellular with specialized tissues and organ systems.

5. Lesson Summary

  • All living things share the seven major characteristics: organization, metabolism, homeostasis, growth, reproduction, response, and adaptation.
  • Nonliving things may show some but never all characteristics of life.
  • Cells are the basic unit of life — every living organism is made up of one or more cells.
