Introduction to Genetics and Heredity

Genetics is the study of how traits are passed from parents to offspring. This lesson introduces the core vocabulary and simple models (like Mendel's pea experiments) that explain why children resemble their parents.

1. What is Genetics?

Genetics is the science of heredity and variation. It explains how characteristics (traits) such as eye color, seed shape, or height are transmitted across generations.

Key idea

A trait is determined by genes — segments of DNA that carry instructions for making proteins. Each gene can have different forms called alleles.

2. Genotype and Phenotype

Genotype = the genetic makeup (the alleles an organism carries). Phenotype = the observable traits (what you can see).

Activity 1: Genotype → Phenotype

Given the gene for flower color: allele R (red, dominant) and allele r (white, recessive). Match the genotype to phenotype:

  • RR → ?
  • Rr → ?
  • rr → ?
  1. RR → Red (homozygous dominant)
  2. Rr → Red (heterozygous; dominant allele masks recessive)
  3. rr → White (homozygous recessive)

3. Genes, Alleles & Chromosomes

Genes live on chromosomes — long DNA molecules packaged with proteins. Most organisms inherit one set of chromosomes from each parent, so genes usually come in pairs (two alleles).

DNA → gene → chromosome: the nested hierarchy.

4. Gregor Mendel — short story

Gregor Mendel (mid-1800s) carefully crossed pea plants and tracked traits (shape, color, seed texture). From patterns he discovered simple mathematical rules of inheritance — now known as Mendel's laws.

Activity 2: Mendel's experiment (quick question)

Mendel observed that crossing pure-breeding tall plants with pure-breeding short plants produced all tall offspring (first generation). Which allele do you expect is dominant?

Answer: The allele for tallness is dominant — it masks the short allele in hybrids.

Short explainer

5. Predicting inheritance: Punnett square (monohybrid)

A Punnett square is a simple grid that shows how parental alleles combine in offspring. Example: cross Rr × Rr (heterozygote × heterozygote).

  • Possible gametes: R or r (from each parent)
  • Offspring genotypes: RR, Rr, rR, rr → genotype ratio 1 : 2 : 1
  • Phenotype (if R is dominant): 3 red : 1 white

Activity 3: Practice — fill the square

Predict the phenotype ratio when a homozygous dominant (RR) is crossed with a heterozygote (Rr).

Answer: All offspring receive at least one R: genotypes = RR and Rr → phenotype = 100% red (dominant).

6. Why genetics matters

Genetics explains inherited diseases, crop breeding, biodiversity, and how traits spread in populations. Modern genetics extends Mendel's rules to complex traits and molecular mechanisms.

Activity 4: Quick reflection

Give one real-world example where understanding heredity is useful (medicine, agriculture, conservation).

  • Medicine: Genetic testing for inherited conditions (e.g., cystic fibrosis).
  • Agriculture: Breeding crops for disease resistance and yield.
  • Conservation: Managing breeding in endangered species to maintain diversity.

7. Lesson summary

  • Genetics studies how traits pass from parents to offspring via genes and alleles.
  • Genotype = genetic makeup; phenotype = observable trait.
  • Mendel's pea experiments revealed predictable inheritance patterns that form the foundation for classical genetics.
