Lesson 1: Introduction to Genetics and Heredity

Genetics is the study of how traits and characteristics are passed from parents to their offspring. It helps explain why we look similar to our parents yet still have our own unique features. In this lesson, we’ll explore the foundation of heredity — from genes and alleles to the pioneering work of Gregor Mendel.

1. What is Genetics?

Genetics is the branch of biology that studies how traits are inherited. It explains how the information stored in the DNA of parents is passed down to their children.

Every living organism carries a set of instructions called genes that determine its features — like eye color, blood type, flower color, or height.

2. Heredity and Variation

Heredity is the process through which traits are passed from parents to offspring. This explains why children often share physical resemblances with their parents.

However, not all offspring are identical. Differences among individuals of the same species are called variations. These variations make populations more adaptable and are the foundation for evolution.

Activity 1: Family resemblance

List three physical traits you share with your parents or relatives. Then list one or two that are unique to you.

Example: Same brown eyes and curly hair as my mother; taller than both parents.

3. Genes, Alleles, and Traits

Traits are controlled by genes, which are small segments of DNA found on chromosomes. Each gene can have different forms called alleles. One allele may be dominant (stronger expression) while the other is recessive (masked in presence of dominant).

Each gene occupies a specific position on a chromosome and may have alternate forms (alleles).

The combination of alleles that an organism carries is its genotype. The physical expression of those alleles (like hair color or height) is its phenotype.

Activity 2: Determining phenotype

If the gene for seed color has alleles “Y” (yellow, dominant) and “y” (green, recessive), what color seeds will these plants have?
Genotypes: YY, Yy, yy

  • YY → Yellow (dominant, homozygous dominant)
  • Yy → Yellow (dominant, heterozygous)
  • yy → Green (recessive, homozygous recessive)

4. Mendel: The Father of Genetics

Gregor Mendel - Father of Genetics

Gregor Mendel was an Austrian monk who, in the mid-1800s, performed experiments on pea plants. By carefully cross-breeding plants and observing traits like color and shape, he discovered predictable patterns of inheritance.

His observations led to what we now call the Laws of Inheritance — which explain how traits are passed down from generation to generation.

Mendel’s pea plant experiments laid the foundation of modern genetics.

Activity 3: What did Mendel discover?

Mendel noticed that when he crossed a pure tall plant (TT) with a pure short plant (tt), all the offspring were tall (Tt). What does this tell us about the alleles for height?

The allele for tallness (T) is dominant over the allele for shortness (t).

Lesson Summary

  • Genetics is the study of how traits are passed from parents to offspring.
  • Heredity ensures the transmission of genetic information across generations.
  • Genes are segments of DNA, and alleles are their alternative forms.
  • Genotype refers to genetic makeup; phenotype is the physical expression.
  • Gregor Mendel discovered the basic principles of inheritance through his pea plant experiments.
