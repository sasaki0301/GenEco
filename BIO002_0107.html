Chapter 1 — Foundations of Biology

This chapter introduces the major foundations of biology: organization, characteristics of life, nutrition & gas exchange, irritability & adaptations, excretion & feedback, and reproduction. Use the buttons below to jump to lesson pages or take the chapter assessment.

Lesson 1 — Levels of Biological Organization

Life is structured in nested levels: atom → molecule → organelle → cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism → population → community → ecosystem → biosphere. Each higher level has emergent properties that cannot be predicted solely from the lower level.

Activity — Quick check

  1. Why is a tissue more than simply a group of identical cells?
    Tissues are organized groups of different but functionally cooperating cells; emergent function (e.g., contraction in muscle tissue) arises from the coordinated action.
  2. Give one example of an emergent property and the two levels it connects.
    Example: Conscious behavior (emergent) arises from networks of neurons (cell → tissue/organ system).

Lesson 2 — Characteristics of Living Things

Living organisms display organization, metabolism, homeostasis, growth & development, reproduction, response to stimuli, and adaptation through evolution. Nonliving objects may show some traits but never the full set.

Activity — Multiple questions (2)

  1. Which characteristic explains why organisms heal after injury?
    Growth & repair (cell division and tissue regeneration)
  2. How does homeostasis differ from simple response to environment?
    Homeostasis maintains internal variables within narrow ranges via regulated feedback; simple responses are single actions to immediate stimuli.

Lesson 3 — Nutrition & Gas Exchange

Autotrophs make food (photosynthesis), heterotrophs obtain nutrients externally (holozoic, saprophytic, parasitic). Gas exchange supplies O₂ for respiration and removes CO₂ — plants use stomata; animals use lungs/gills/skin.

Activity — Short questions (3)

  1. Define saprophytic nutrition and give an organism example.
    Saprophytic: organisms secrete enzymes onto dead matter and absorb dissolved nutrients (example: fungi).
  2. Why are alveoli effective for gas exchange?
    Large surface area, thin walls, rich blood supply — favor diffusion.
  3. What structural difference helps xerophytes conserve water?
    Reduced leaf area or spines, thick cuticle, sunken stomata, water-storage tissues.

Lesson 4 — Irritability & Adaptability

Plants respond primarily by tropisms (phototropism, geotropism, hydrotropism, thigmotropism, thermotropism — positive or negative). Animals respond quickly by taxis (phototaxis, chemotaxis, aerotaxis, geotaxis, thigmotaxis) and reflexes. Adaptations are structural, behavioral, or physiological.

Activity — Choose & explain (2)

  1. Give one plant tropism and specify whether it is positive or negative by example.
    Example: Roots growing down = positive geotropism; shoots grow up = negative geotropism.
  2. List one behavioral adaptation in animals and explain its survival value.
    Example: Migration in birds — avoids harsh conditions and accesses seasonal resources.

Lesson 5 — Excretion & Feedback Mechanisms

Excretion removes metabolic waste (CO₂, urea, bile pigments, salts). Animals use lungs, kidneys, liver, skin; plants use stomata/transpiration, guttation, secretion, storage and shedding. Negative feedback restores balance; positive feedback amplifies processes (childbirth, clotting).

Activity — Apply (3)

  1. Explain how ADH participates in water balance.
    ADH increases water reabsorption in kidney collecting ducts, reducing urine volume — negative feedback to maintain osmolarity.
  2. Give one plant excretion mechanism and why it helps the plant.
    Guttation removes excess water/solutes at leaf margins, helping relieve root pressure.
  3. Give one example of positive feedback and explain its endpoint.
    Childbirth: oxytocin amplifies contractions until delivery removes the stimulus (birth) and feedback stops.

Lesson 6 — Differentiating Plant & Animal Reproduction

Asexual reproduction (binary fission, budding, fragmentation, vegetative propagation, spores) produces clones. Sexual reproduction uses meiosis, gametes and fertilization to generate variation. Many plants show alternation of generations (gametophyte ↔ sporophyte).

Activity — Check (3)

  1. What is double fertilization and why is it important?
    In angiosperms, one sperm fertilizes egg (zygote) and the other fuses with polar nuclei to form endosperm — provides nutritive tissue for embryo.
  2. Why is alternation of generations significant in plants?
    It separates sexual (gametophyte) and spore-producing (sporophyte) phases, allowing specialization and life-cycle flexibility.
  3. Give one advantage and one disadvantage of asexual reproduction.
    Advantage: quick, no mate needed; disadvantage: low genetic diversity.
