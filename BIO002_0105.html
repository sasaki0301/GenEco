Excretion & Feedback Mechanisms

Excretion is the process by which organisms remove metabolic wastes and maintain internal chemical balance. Feedback mechanisms (negative and positive) regulate internal conditions to keep organisms healthy.

1. What is Excretion?

Excretion removes harmful metabolic wastes (e.g., urea, CO₂, excess salts, bile pigments) and helps maintain water, ion, and pH balance (homeostasis). Different organisms use different organs and processes depending on their structure and environment.

2. Excretion in Animals — major organs & processes

Animals typically use specialized organs to filter and eliminate wastes:

  • Lungs — remove gaseous wastes, mainly carbon dioxide and water vapor (exhalation).
  • Kidneys — filter blood to form urine (removing urea, excess salts, water) and help regulate blood volume, electrolytes and pH.
  • Liver — transforms and detoxifies substances (makes urea from ammonia, produces bile to excrete bilirubin and excess cholesterol).
  • Skin — excretes small amounts of waste via sweat (water, salts, some urea) and helps temperature regulation.
  • Intestine — solid wastes and some pigments (bile pigments) eliminated as feces (note: digestion vs excretion — some waste elimination overlaps).

  1. What waste product is primarily removed by the kidneys?
  2. How does the liver help with excretion (name one process)?
  1. Urea (from protein metabolism) is one of the main nitrogenous wastes filtered by the kidneys.
  2. The liver converts toxic ammonia into urea (the urea cycle) and secretes waste products into bile (e.g., bilirubin) for elimination in the intestine.

3. Excretion in Plants — how plants eliminate wastes

Plants do not have organs like kidneys, but they still remove or isolate wastes using several processes:

  • Respiration & transpiration: CO₂ and water vapor diffuse out through stomata (gaseous waste).
  • Guttation: water with dissolved salts is exuded at leaf margins (drops on leaf tips) — a way to remove excess water/solutes.
  • Storage & sequestration: Plants store toxic compounds in vacuoles, bark, or as crystals (e.g., calcium oxalate) to prevent harm.
  • Exudates & resins: Some wastes are secreted as resins, gums, or saps and later shed (e.g., latex, tannins).
  • Leaf abscission: Plants remove wastes by shedding leaves, bark, or other tissues (dead tissue carries waste away).

Activity 2 (Plants):

  1. How do stomata help a plant excrete wastes?
  2. Give one example of how plants store or isolate harmful substances.
  1. Stomata allow gases (CO₂ out during respiration, O₂ in during photosynthesis) and water vapor to diffuse — this removes gaseous wastes and helps transport dissolved substances via transpiration stream.
  2. Plants sequester harmful substances in vacuoles or cell walls, or store them as crystals (e.g., calcium oxalate); some are secreted into resins or latex and later shed.

4. Feedback Mechanisms — Keeping internal balance

Organisms maintain stable internal conditions using feedback systems. A negative feedback opposes a change (restores balance). A positive feedback amplifies a change (drives a process to completion).

5. Negative Feedback — examples & explanation

Negative feedback keeps variables near a set point. It is the most common control mechanism in biology.

  • Thermoregulation: If body temperature rises, sweat production and vasodilation cool the body; if it falls, shivering and vasoconstriction conserve heat.
  • Blood glucose regulation: High blood glucose → pancreas releases insulin → cells uptake glucose → blood glucose falls. Low blood glucose → pancreas releases glucagon → liver releases glucose.
  • Blood calcium levels: Low Ca²⁺ → parathyroid hormone increases bone resorption and renal reabsorption; high Ca²⁺ → calcitonin reduces bone resorption.
  • Osmoregulation: Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) controls water reabsorption in kidneys to maintain osmotic balance.

Activity 3 (Negative feedback):

  1. Explain how insulin participates in a negative feedback loop.
  2. Give one negative feedback example that helps regulate body temperature.
  1. When blood glucose rises after a meal, β-cells in the pancreas secrete insulin, which promotes glucose uptake and storage, lowering blood glucose toward the set point — this reduces the original stimulus.
  2. If body temperature increases, sweat glands produce sweat and blood vessels near the skin dilate (vasodilation), increasing heat loss and bringing temperature back toward normal.

6. Positive Feedback — examples & when it's used

Positive feedback amplifies a response and is useful when a rapid, decisive change is required. It often ends when an external event stops it or when the process completes.

  • Childbirth: Stretching of the cervix stimulates oxytocin release → stronger uterine contractions → more cervical stretch → more oxytocin (loop continues until birth).
  • Blood clotting: Platelet adhesion releases chemicals that attract more platelets, rapidly building a clot to stop bleeding.
  • Lactation (part of milk ejection): Suckling stimulates oxytocin release → milk ejection → continued suckling reinforces signal.

Activity 4 (Positive feedback):

  1. Why is positive feedback useful during childbirth?
  2. Give an example of positive feedback in blood clotting.
  1. Positive feedback during childbirth ramps up uterine contractions quickly to move labor forward and ensure the baby is delivered — amplification continues until birth completes (process ends when the stimulus is removed).
  2. When a blood vessel is damaged, platelets adhere and release chemicals (e.g., ADP, thromboxane) that recruit and activate more platelets — this amplifies clot formation until the vessel is sealed.

9. Lesson Summary

  • Excretion removes metabolic wastes and maintains internal balance (water, ions, pH).
  • Animals use lungs, kidneys, liver, skin, and intestine to remove different wastes; plants use stomata, guttation, secretion, storage and shedding.
  • Negative feedback opposes changes and maintains stability; positive feedback amplifies processes and is used when rapid completion is needed.
  • Both excretion and feedback are essential for organism survival and proper functioning.
