Lesson 3: Mendel’s Second Law — Law of Segregation

Mendel’s Law of Segregation explains how traits are inherited from parents to offspring. It states that each organism carries two alleles for each trait, and these alleles separate during gamete formation — so each gamete receives only one allele from each pair.

1. What is Segregation?

The Law of Segregation states that the two alleles for each trait separate during the formation of gametes. This ensures that offspring receive one allele from each parent.

Each gamete gets only one allele for a given trait (Y or y).

For example, a pea plant with the genotype Yy (yellow and green alleles) can form gametes that contain either Y or y, but not both at the same time.

2. Gamete Formation and Allele Separation

During meiosis, the process that forms gametes (sperm and egg cells), the pair of alleles separate so that each gamete receives only one allele per gene.

When fertilization occurs, the offspring again have two alleles — one from the mother and one from the father.

Activity 1: Apply What You Learned

A pea plant has genotype Tt. What kinds of gametes can it produce according to the Law of Segregation?

It produces two types of gametes — one carrying T and one carrying t.

5. Summary — Law of Segregation

  • Each trait is controlled by two alleles — one from each parent.
  • Alleles separate during gamete formation (meiosis).
  • Each gamete carries only one allele for each trait.
  • When gametes unite during fertilization, they restore the pair of alleles in offspring.

Key Terms and Simple Meanings

TermMeaning
AlleleDifferent versions of the same gene (e.g., T or t).
SegregationSeparation of alleles during gamete formation.
GameteReproductive cell (sperm or egg) carrying one allele per gene.
Genotypic RatioProportion of genotypes (e.g., 1:2:1).
Phenotypic RatioProportion of physical traits (e.g., 3 tall : 1 short).
